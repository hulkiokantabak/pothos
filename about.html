POTHOS

a garden of appointments

You sow a seed with a small gesture, and the garden tells you exactly when it will open: tomorrow, in a week, in a season. Nothing you do can hurry it. It does not need watering and cannot be harmed; it grows on its own clock whether you watch or not. The bloom is brief, on the date it named, and then it goes to seed. If you were there while it was open, the record says witnessed. If you were not, it says: it opened while you were away. Both records are kept with equal care. They are not the same record.

That is the whole piece. The rest of this page says what it is about, and then discloses every deliberate choice in it, because a machine that asks you to wait owes you the truth about how the waiting works.

The word

The word this piece was built from is desire. The Greeks kept two words where English keeps one. In Plato's Cratylus, Socrates separates himeros, the pull toward what is present, from pothos (πόθος), the longing for what is absent — for what is elsewhere. Homer uses pothos for the ache toward the missing and the dead. This garden is built inside the second word: everything in it is either not yet, or no longer, and the only thing that closes the distance is time, which does not take requests.

Pothos is not pathos, though the letters nearly rhyme: pathos comes from paskhein, to suffer — what is done to you; pothos from potheō, to long for — what you reach toward. In the author's own language the word is hasret.

The name — pothos

Etymology. Πόθος belongs to the verb potheō — to long for, to miss, to feel the absence of. Most philologists trace it — not with certainty — to an old Indo-European root of asking and praying, the root behind Old Irish's own word for prayer: at bottom, pothos is a petition aimed at what is not here. Greek kept a whole family where English keeps one word — eros for desire with a face, himeros for the pull of the present, pothos for the ache of the absent — and Greek art personified all three: on the vases Pothos stands in Aphrodite's train beside Eros and Himeros, and the sculptor Skopas carved him famously leaning, weight given to the empty air he looks toward. Arrian keeps using the word for Alexander: a pothos seized him — to see the far side of a river, the end of an ocean — longing as the engine that drags a life past every horizon. And Theophrastus lists a garland-flower the Greeks actually called pothos; later writers say it was planted on graves. Longing was a flower two thousand years before Linnaeus latinized a Sinhala word into the houseplant, and the coincidence this piece builds on turns out to be older than the pun.

Philosophy. That the Greeks split desire by presence is the whole idea this garden grows from: it makes desire an index of distance, and distance, on the web, survives only as time. Plato has Diotima teach that eros is neither having nor lacking but the between — a reaching, the child of Poverty and Resource — and pothos is that reaching held open when the object cannot be reached at all: Homer's word for the ache toward the missing and the dead, the register Anne Carson calls the bittersweet. Desire in this key is not a problem the world solves by delivery; it is a way of standing in time, faced toward a date. So the garden gives the word its plainest possible body — a seed, an appointment, a window, a record — and refuses everything that would collapse the distance early: no hurrying, no reminders, no color before its hour. Where the lack- tradition says possession kills desire, the garden answers otherwise: the bloom is real and brief, the record keeps it, and the next seed is offered, never pushed. And where the modern machine treats your wanting as a crop, this one lets it stay a petition — which is what the root always said it was.

A register, not a definition

This garden plays desire in one register — desire as distance, the lack-tradition that runs from Plato through Anne Carson. It is a register, not a definition. Spinoza answers that desire is the very striving a being is; Deleuze answers that desire builds rather than misses. Those are live rivals, not defeated ones. If the garden has a metaphysical home it is Diotima's: eros as the between — the reaching, not the hole.

A confession, unhedged: this garden manufactures the desire it then keeps faith with. You did not want this bloom before the machine told you it would come. Every made thing does this; the difference claimed here is not innocence. The making is disclosed, the schedule is fixed, and nothing is harvested.

A second confession, narrower: the word was chosen with care — desire was cut down to pothos, longing for the absent, out of a word that also holds appetite and ambition — but the form was not chosen so freely. The garden, the seed, the waiting bloom was the most fluent shape its maker had for that longing, not the only honest one; the same thesis could have been a letter, a tide, a held breath. The plant is named here as a fluency, not a necessity, so that its prettiness is never taken for the argument.

The line under the whole design is this: the boundary between anticipation and compulsion is disclosed fixed timing versus farmed uncertainty. Anticipation is still dopamine, but the piece rests nothing on that — its own cited science (see the colophon) finds a fully-expected reward pays almost nothing at delivery; the wait is a lever the piece names, not a circuit it leans on. What it claims is only this: no scarcity, no queue, no audience, and nothing accrues.

The neighbors, named

The tamagotchi punished absence with death: retention by guilt. Forest made the plant a token of your discipline: abstention gamified. Viridi already made the placid real-time plant. What remains here is what none of them kept: an exact, disclosed appointment; a window that can truly be missed; and a plant that needs nothing from you at all. The plant does not need you. The visits are for you: attention without efficacy.

The two doors

See it now. Every growing plant will show you, while you hold the door open, a drawing of the bloom to come — the form complete, the color not shown. Lacan says you can never see the object of your desire, because it does not exist. This machine disagrees on purpose: the drawing shows you nearly everything, and whether the wanting survives the seeing is the experiment. Taking the door is recorded, once, as a plain fact: seen early. The record is memory, not judgment. Nothing about the plant changes.

Sow one for someone. A seed can be sown for another person and sent as a link. The link carries only the seed's facts — its nature, its dates. No name goes with it, nothing comes back, and the sender is never told whether it was taken in. A letter, not a network. It starts growing the moment it is sown, so it ages in transit; some gifts arrive already pressed, and the record says so.

Every rig, disclosed

  • The gesture. One gene of the seed is legible: a longer hold sows a taller plant (the hold is read up to two and a half seconds; there is nothing to grind). The rest of the seed's nature is taken from the fine grain of the release moment. The same gesture always grows the same plant; there is no randomness at any later step, and no two reveals of the same seed can differ. Sowing by keyboard, switch, or voice is two presses rather than one held one; the gap between them is read the same way, so the taller-plant gene reflects that gap — worth knowing if your device makes timing hard to control.
  • The placement door. After you hold a seed you choose where its opening lands: the hour you sowed it, with the morning, or with the evening. The morning band lands between 08:00 and 11:00, the evening band between 19:00 and 22:00 — up to three hours of minutes, taken from the seed — on the nearest such hour to the wait's end, which can sit a little before it or after it. The exact time is printed on each choice before you commit. This exists so that arithmetic never books your bloom for 03:00 unless you chose that hour.
  • Growth is staged. Growth is a pure function of the seed and the clock, quantized into visible stages (a Day plant has six; a Season plant, twelve) so that two visits a day apart look different. Between stages the plant holds still. Dated notes on each plant record when each stage arrived; they are computed from the clock, never stored.
  • The rehearsal. The Rehearsal seed runs the whole arc in minutes and is drawn in line only — no color. It is the map of the wait, not the wait; most visitors will only ever see the map, and the piece accepts that. Color exists in this garden only after a real wait, or live through a gift link while its bloom is open — except a seam of it where the bud splits, in the last stage before opening. The rehearsal shelf keeps only the most recent pressing.
  • The pressing shows what you saw. A bloom that was open while you were here is pressed open-faced, with its color quieted the way pressed flowers fade. A bloom you saw only through the drawing door is pressed as that drawing — so foreseeing also upgrades what a missed bloom's pressing can show; the piece accepts that trade. A bloom never seen is pressed as the seed-head that remained — and its words still tell you everything it was. Images are testimony of presence; the words always carry the whole truth. Nothing counts, sums, or scores any of this.
  • The clock is yours. The garden trusts your device's clock. If your clock goes backward, the garden keeps the later hour rather than let a plant regress. Nothing stops you from setting your clock forward and forcing the season. You would see the bloom; you would also know how you got it. One edge, stated plainly: the garden cannot un-know a later day. If you force the clock forward, it keeps that furthest hour until real time catches up, and every appointment in between will already have passed. If a change of clocks (daylight saving) falls inside a wait, the printed hour shifts with it, and the garden says so when you sow.
  • Browsers forget. Your garden lives in this browser and nowhere else. Some browsers quietly clear storage after a week of absence — which would break a Season's promise — so every real sowing hands you a seed line: a short address that carries the seed's whole future. Keep it anywhere; opening it in any browser regrows the plant with its appointments intact. Only the facts about your visits cannot travel with it.
  • The date is yours to keep. Each plant offers its window as a calendar file. Your calendar, your rules; the garden itself will never remind you, message you, or mark its tab. Memory is the only summons.
  • The hour tint. The page's ground shifts very slightly with your local hour. It is a tint, not a scene.
  • One voice for dates. The garden speaks its dates in a single voice — day before month, a 24-hour clock — whatever your device's locale. One voice, one calendar; the hours themselves are always your own timezone's.
  • The petition, made literal. Because the piece calls itself a petition and admits browsers forget, at your first real sowing it asks the browser's own petition — to mark this garden as storage worth keeping — and prints the browser's plain answer on the card. Some browsers show their own small dialog when this is asked; that dialog is the browser's, not the garden's. A persisted garden is far less likely to be cleared out from under a Season.
  • The landlord. There is no server of this piece's own, and nothing you do is transmitted. But the pages are served by GitHub Pages, which — like any web host — can see the address of whoever asks for a page. That is the one set of eyes on the road to the garden; nothing inside the garden reaches them.
  • The register, and its limit. This is a piece about longing — including the ache toward the missing and the dead. A Season is ninety real days, in which a life can change. The garden will never contact you, summon you, or ask how you are; it is not a companion and cannot know how you are doing. If the wait ever asks more of you than a flower should, close the tab — nothing is lost, and nothing is watching. The record keeps itself either way.
  • The gift link is a letter, and letters can be photocopied. Anyone holding the link can regrow the same seed. The piece does not pretend scarcity it does not have.

What the garden refuses

No account, no server, no analytics beyond an anonymous page-view count (see the colophon), no notifications, no streaks, no scores, no daily anything, no urgency, no countdown, and no mechanism anywhere by which visiting more often changes anything. Checking early shows you the truth and nothing extra. Absence is never punished; the one thing at stake in returning is whether you were there.

The registers not taken

Desire has species this garden deliberately does not play: appetite and its literal objects; mimetic desire in its rivalrous form — no trending, no visible wanting of others (though the gift is mimetic desire in its oldest benign form: one person's wanting, pointed at another); the manufactured desire of the feed, refused in the structure rather than in a caption; and the Buddhist answer, which is not to tend desire but to see through it. That last one deserves naming with respect: extinguishing the wanting is a real answer. This piece keeps the wanting, and tends it.
